✓ Clinical Rationales

The client's clinical presentation, including profound muscle weakness, bradycardia, and peaked T-waves on the ECG, combined with a stat lab result showing a potassium level of 6.8 mEq/L, overwhelmingly points to hyperkalemia. The pathophysiology involves excess extracellular potassium, which alters the cardiac myocyte resting membrane potential. This change initially increases excitability but ultimately impairs repolarization (causing peaked T-waves) and slows conduction, leading to bradycardia, widened QRS, and potentially fatal arrhythmias like ventricular fibrillation or asystole. The client's acute kidney injury (AKI) is the primary cause, as the kidneys are unable to excrete potassium. Lisinopril, an ACE inhibitor, exacerbates this by inhibiting aldosterone, which normally promotes potassium excretion.
