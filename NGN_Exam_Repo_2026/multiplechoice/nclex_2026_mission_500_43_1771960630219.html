✓ Clinical Rationales

Appropriate interventions for a patient with acute MI include administering oxygen to improve oxygenation to ischemic tissues, administering prescribed medications such as morphine for pain and nitrates for vasodilation to reduce cardiac workload, monitoring cardiac rhythm for dysrhythmias, and ensuring bedrest to reduce myocardial oxygen demand. Administering a Fleet enema is not typically indicated in the acute management of MI and may cause vagal stimulation leading to bradycardia. Encouraging ambulation is contraindicated during the acute phase due to the increased oxygen demand it places on the heart. The rationale for administering oxygen is to increase the oxygen supply to the ischemic myocardium, reducing further damage. Morphine reduces pain and anxiety, decreasing myocardial oxygen demand and promoting comfort. Continuous cardiac monitoring is essential to detect and manage potentially life-threatening dysrhythmias. Bedrest reduces myocardial oxygen demand and workload during the acute phase of MI.
