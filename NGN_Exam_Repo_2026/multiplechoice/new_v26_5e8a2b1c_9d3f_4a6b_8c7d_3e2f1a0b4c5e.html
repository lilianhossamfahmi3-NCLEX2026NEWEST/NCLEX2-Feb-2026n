✓ Clinical Rationales

Administering calcium gluconate is the priority intervention. The patient's elevated potassium level (6.8 mEq/L) poses a significant risk for cardiac arrhythmias. Calcium gluconate does not lower potassium levels but stabilizes the myocardial cell membrane, reducing the risk of arrhythmia until other interventions can lower the potassium. Given the lethargy, decreased urine output, and elevated BUN/Creatinine, AKI superimposed on CKD is highly likely, making immediate action to protect the heart crucial. The effect of hyperkalemia on the heart can manifest as peaked T waves, widened QRS complexes, and ultimately, cardiac arrest. Calcium gluconate provides immediate cardioprotection by increasing the threshold potential, thus preventing arrhythmias. This intervention buys time for other potassium-lowering treatments to take effect.
