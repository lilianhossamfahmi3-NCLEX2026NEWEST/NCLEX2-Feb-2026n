The patient's clinical picture—pneumonia, persistent hypotension despite fluid resuscitation (MAP < 65 mmHg), tachycardia, tachypnea, confusion, and elevated lactate—is classic for distributive shock, specifically septic shock. The underlying pathophysiology is a massive inflammatory response to infection, causing widespread vasodilation and increased capillary permeability. This leads to a relative hypovolemia and a drastic drop in systemic vascular resistance (SVR). The priority is to correct the life-threatening hypotension. Since the patient is fluid-refractory, vasopressors (e.g., norepinephrine) are required to induce vasoconstriction, increase SVR, and raise the MAP to a level (≥ 65 mmHg) that ensures adequate perfusion to vital organs. Urine output is a critical, real-time indicator of renal and systemic perfusion.
