✓ Clinical Rationales

Administering intravenous fluids is crucial in HHS to correct dehydration and restore circulatory volume. The patient's low blood pressure, elevated heart rate, and elevated serum osmolality indicate severe dehydration. Monitoring serum potassium levels is essential because insulin shifts potassium into cells, potentially causing hypokalemia, which can lead to cardiac arrhythmias. The patient's potassium is already low at 3.0 mEq/L. Initiating a continuous insulin infusion is necessary to lower the blood glucose levels gradually. Rapid reduction can lead to cerebral edema. Monitoring neurological status frequently is vital to detect early signs of cerebral edema or other neurological complications associated with HHS, such as seizures or altered mental status. Assessing urine output hourly is important to evaluate the effectiveness of fluid resuscitation and monitor kidney function. The patient's current urine output is low, indicating inadequate perfusion.
