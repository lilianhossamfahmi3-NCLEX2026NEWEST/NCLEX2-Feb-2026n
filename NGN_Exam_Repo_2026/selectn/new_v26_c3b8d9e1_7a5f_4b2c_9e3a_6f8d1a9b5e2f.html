✓ Clinical Rationales

Initiating intravenous fluid resuscitation with 0.9% NaCl is crucial to address the patient's dehydration and hypotension. DKA causes osmotic diuresis, leading to significant fluid loss, exacerbating hypovolemia and electrolyte imbalances. Starting a continuous insulin infusion will help lower the blood glucose level and correct the metabolic acidosis, gradually shifting the body back to a normal metabolic state. The initial rate should be guided by hospital protocol and patient response, with frequent monitoring. Administering potassium chloride is essential to correct hypokalemia, a common complication of DKA and insulin therapy. Insulin shifts potassium intracellularly, potentially worsening hypokalemia and leading to cardiac arrhythmias. Monitoring the patient's neurological status is crucial to detect changes in level of consciousness and signs of cerebral edema, a rare but serious complication of DKA treatment, particularly in elderly patients. Frequent blood glucose monitoring (every 1-2 hours) is essential to adjust the insulin infusion rate and prevent hypoglycemia or rebound hyperglycemia.
