✓ Clinical Rationales

ARDS is characterized by diffuse alveolar damage, leading to increased permeability of the alveolar-capillary membrane. This results in pulmonary edema and the formation of hyaline membranes, which impair gas exchange. Radiographically, this manifests as bilateral infiltrates, diffuse haziness, and ground-glass opacities, especially in the lower and peripheral lung fields. These findings distinguish ARDS from other pulmonary conditions like pneumonia, which typically presents with lobar consolidation, or pleural effusions, which involve fluid accumulation in the pleural space. The key pathophysiological feature driving these radiographic changes is the inflammatory process causing protein-rich fluid leakage into the alveoli and interstitial spaces. The alveolar damage and fluid accumulation reduce lung compliance and increase pulmonary vascular resistance, leading to hypoxemia and increased work of breathing.
