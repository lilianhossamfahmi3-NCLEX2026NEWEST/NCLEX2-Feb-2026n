A 72-year-old female is admitted to the ICU with Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Syndrome (HHS). Based on the assessment data, which of the following interventions are MOST appropriate? Select all that apply.

✓ Clinical Rationales

Administer 0.9% Normal Saline intravenously: HHS causes severe dehydration due to osmotic diuresis. Rapid rehydration is crucial to restore intravascular volume and improve perfusion to vital organs. Normal saline is preferred initially due to the patient's hypernatremia. Initiate continuous IV insulin infusion: While no ketones are present, the extremely elevated glucose level requires insulin to facilitate glucose uptake by cells and lower serum glucose. A continuous infusion allows for precise titration. Monitor serum potassium levels closely: HHS can cause potassium shifts. Insulin administration drives potassium into cells, potentially leading to hypokalemia. Replacement is essential to prevent cardiac arrhythmias. Implement seizure precautions: The combination of hyperosmolality and electrolyte imbalances increases the risk of seizures. Taking precautions to prevent injury is paramount. Initiate continuous cardiac monitoring: Electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, and potential for arrhythmias necessitate continuous cardiac monitoring to detect and respond to any changes promptly.
