✓ Clinical Rationales

The correct order is: 1. **Administer calcium gluconate:** Calcium gluconate stabilizes the cardiac membrane, protecting against the effects of hyperkalemia on the heart, preventing arrhythmias. It does not lower potassium levels but provides immediate cardiac protection. 2. **Administer regular insulin with dextrose:** Insulin shifts potassium into the cells, lowering serum potassium levels. Dextrose is given concurrently to prevent hypoglycemia, as insulin drives glucose into cells along with potassium. 3. **Administer sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate):** Kayexalate binds to potassium in the GI tract, promoting its excretion through the feces. This is a slower-acting method for potassium removal and is not suitable for acute, severe hyperkalemia. 4. **Initiate continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) if other measures are ineffective and the patient has persistent, severe hyperkalemia:** CRRT is used as a last resort when other measures fail to reduce potassium levels, especially in patients with AKI or CKD. It provides continuous potassium removal.
