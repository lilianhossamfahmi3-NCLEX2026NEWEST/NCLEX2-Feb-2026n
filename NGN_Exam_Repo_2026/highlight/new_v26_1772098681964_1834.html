✓ Clinical Rationales

A potassium level of 6.8 mEq/L indicates hyperkalemia, a life-threatening electrolyte imbalance that can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and arrest. In AKI, the kidneys' ability to excrete potassium is impaired, leading to its accumulation in the bloodstream. This increased extracellular potassium depolarizes the cell membranes, affecting cardiac conduction and muscle function. Immediate intervention, such as administering calcium gluconate, insulin with glucose, or sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate), is required to stabilize the cardiac membrane and lower the potassium level. ECG monitoring is crucial to detect early signs of hyperkalemia, such as peaked T waves or widened QRS complexes.
