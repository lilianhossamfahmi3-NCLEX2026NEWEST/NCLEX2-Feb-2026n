✓ Clinical Rationales

Pulmonary embolism (PE) occurs when a blood clot, most commonly originating from a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the legs, travels to the pulmonary arteries and obstructs blood flow. This obstruction leads to a cascade of physiological consequences. Sudden-onset pleuritic chest pain arises from the inflammatory response and irritation of the pleura due to the embolism. Dyspnea results from impaired gas exchange as the blocked pulmonary arteries reduce the surface area available for oxygen and carbon dioxide exchange. Anxiety is a common response to the sensation of breathlessness and chest pain. Tachypnea (RR 32) and tachycardia (HR 122) are compensatory mechanisms to improve oxygen delivery to tissues. Hypoxemia (SpO2 86%) reflects the ventilation-perfusion mismatch caused by the blocked pulmonary arteries. A dry, nonproductive cough can occur due to irritation of the airways. Feeling faint (syncope or near-syncope) suggests significant hemodynamic compromise. Tachycardia is a direct result of the body attempting to compensate for decreased oxygenation. Crackles in the lung bases may indicate pulmonary edema secondary to increased pulmonary vascular resistance. Hemoptysis results from pulmonary infarction and alveolar hemorrhage. Distended neck veins indicate right ventricular strain and increased central venous pressure due to the obstruction in the pulmonary circulation. Unilateral calf edema, warmth, and tenderness strongly suggest a DVT, the likely source of the PE.
