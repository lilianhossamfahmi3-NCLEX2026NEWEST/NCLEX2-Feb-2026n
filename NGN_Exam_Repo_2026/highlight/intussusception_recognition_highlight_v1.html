✓ Clinical Rationales

Intussusception is a condition where one part of the intestine (the intussusceptum) telescopes into another (the intussuscipiens), leading to bowel obstruction, inflammation, and potential ischemia. The highlighted findings are classic indicators. Sudden, episodic abdominal pain, manifested as inconsolable crying and drawing knees to the chest, results from intermittent bowel contractions as the intestine attempts to push the obstruction forward. 'Currant jelly' stools (dark red and jelly-like) are caused by mucosal edema, venous engorgement, and bleeding within the intussuscepted segment. A sausage-shaped mass in the upper right quadrant is the palpable intussusception itself. Bilious vomiting suggests bowel obstruction proximal to the ileocecal valve. Lethargy between episodes can indicate intermittent pain relief or developing hypovolemia. Abdominal tenderness and distention are signs of inflammation and potential peritonitis. Tachycardia (HR 162 bpm) and pale, cool skin are compensatory mechanisms for hypovolemia and potential shock due to fluid shifts and blood loss into the bowel.
