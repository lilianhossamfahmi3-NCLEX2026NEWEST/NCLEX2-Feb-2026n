✓ Clinical Rationales

The highlighted findings indicate acute respiratory failure. Leaning forward (tripod position) maximizes lung expansion. Tachypnea (RR 32) reflects the body's attempt to compensate for hypoxia and hypercapnia. Accessory muscle use (sternocleidomastoid) indicates increased work of breathing. One-word dyspnea signifies severe breathlessness. An SpO2 of 86% despite supplemental oxygen suggests refractory hypoxemia. Drowsiness and difficulty arousing indicate hypercapnia and CO2 narcosis, impairing neurological function. In COPD, chronic inflammation and structural changes in the lungs (e.g., emphysema) lead to airflow obstruction and impaired gas exchange. During an exacerbation, these issues worsen, leading to increased CO2 retention and decreased oxygen levels. The body's compensatory mechanisms become overwhelmed, resulting in respiratory failure.
