✓ Clinical Rationales

The highlighted findings are indicative of Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS). HHS is a serious complication of type 2 diabetes characterized by severe hyperglycemia (typically >600 mg/dL), hyperosmolarity (serum osmolality >320 mOsm/kg), and dehydration, without significant ketoacidosis. The pathophysiology involves insulin deficiency (relative, not absolute as in DKA), leading to decreased glucose utilization by cells and increased hepatic glucose production. This results in hyperglycemia, causing osmotic diuresis and profound dehydration. The absence of significant ketogenesis differentiates HHS from DKA. * **Increasing confusion:** Cerebral dehydration due to hyperosmolarity leads to altered mental status. * **Dry mucous membranes and sunken eyes:** These are classic signs of dehydration due to osmotic diuresis. * **Flushed and hot skin:** Reflects dehydration and the body's attempt to dissipate heat. * **Hypotension (88/44 mmHg) and tachycardia (124 bpm):** Indicate hypovolemic shock secondary to severe dehydration. The body attempts to compensate for decreased blood volume by increasing heart rate. * **Serum glucose of 1,150 mg/dL:** Confirms severe hyperglycemia, a hallmark of HHS. * **Absent serum ketones:** Distinguishes HHS from DKA, where ketones are present due to lipolysis. * **Serum osmolality of 350 mOsm/kg:** Confirms hyperosmolarity, drawing fluid out of cells and contributing to dehydration. * **Urine output of 15 mL/hr:** Indicates severe dehydration and impaired renal perfusion. Normal urine output is typically >30 mL/hr.
