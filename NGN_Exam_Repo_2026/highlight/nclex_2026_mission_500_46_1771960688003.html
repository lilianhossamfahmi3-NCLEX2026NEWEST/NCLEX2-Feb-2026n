✓ Clinical Rationales

Highlighted findings indicate potential complications of mechanical ventilation. A sudden increase in peak inspiratory pressure (PIP) often signals increased airway resistance (e.g., bronchospasm, mucus plugging, kinked ET tube) or decreased lung compliance (e.g., pneumothorax, pulmonary edema). Hypotension can be a consequence of positive pressure ventilation decreasing venous return and cardiac output, especially in hypovolemic patients. A drop in SpO2 indicates hypoxemia which must be addressed to prevent end-organ damage. High FiO2 levels (>60%) for prolonged periods can lead to oxygen toxicity and absorption atelectasis. While some level of agitation is common, increased agitation may indicate inadequate ventilation, pain, or delirium and requires assessment. Normal tidal volume (Vt) does not necessarily rule out issues as the ideal Vt depends on the patient’s ideal body weight and lung pathology; trending values and clinical context are essential. Plateau pressure is an indicator of alveolar pressure and should be maintained below 30 cm H2O to prevent ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI).
