✓ Clinical Rationales

The highlighted findings are indicative of delirium. Delirium is an acute confusional state characterized by a disturbance in attention and awareness, developing over a short period (hours to days) and tending to fluctuate in severity during the course of the day. The sudden onset of agitation and confusion ('fine at 2000, confused at 0200') points towards an acute process. Inattention is evidenced by the patient's short attention span and inability to follow simple commands. Hallucinations ('large spiders') are a common symptom. Fluctuating levels of consciousness (drowsy to hyper-alert) and disorganized thinking/speech (garbled and incoherent) further support the diagnosis of delirium. The pathophysiology involves neurotransmitter imbalances, particularly acetylcholine deficiency and dopamine excess, leading to impaired neuronal function and disrupted cognitive processes. Inflammation, hypoxia, and metabolic disturbances can also contribute.
