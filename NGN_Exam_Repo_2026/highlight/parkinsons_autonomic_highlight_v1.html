✓ Clinical Rationales

The highlighted findings indicate autonomic nervous system (ANS) dysfunction in Parkinson's disease. * **Lightheaded when standing (Orthostatic Hypotension):** Impaired baroreceptor reflex due to ANS degeneration leads to decreased blood pressure upon standing. The body's compensatory mechanisms (increased heart rate, vasoconstriction) are blunted, causing cerebral hypoperfusion and lightheadedness. * **Oily and greasy skin (Seborrhea):** Dysregulation of the ANS affects sebaceous gland activity, leading to increased sebum production. This is due to imbalanced sympathetic and parasympathetic control over glandular secretions. * **Significant constipation:** Reduced gastrointestinal motility results from impaired parasympathetic innervation of the bowel. Dopamine deficiency in the enteric nervous system also contributes, slowing peristalsis and leading to infrequent bowel movements. * **Excessive salivation and drooling (Sialorrhea):** Impaired swallowing reflexes and reduced autonomic control of salivary glands cause saliva to accumulate in the mouth. The normal automatic swallowing mechanism is impaired. * **Difficulty with bladder emptying:** ANS dysfunction affects bladder muscle control and sphincter function. This can lead to urinary retention, incomplete bladder emptying, and increased risk of urinary tract infections. Impaired detrusor muscle contractility and/or sphincter dyssynergia are the underlying mechanisms. * **Profuse sweating:** Dysregulation of the sympathetic nervous system leads to excessive sweating, even without exertion or elevated body temperature. The thermoregulatory center in the hypothalamus is affected, causing inappropriate activation of sweat glands. * **Coughs frequently and requires a long time to chew and swallow (Dysphagia):** Impaired coordination of the muscles involved in swallowing, partly due to autonomic dysfunction affecting esophageal motility and lower esophageal sphincter function. This increases the risk of aspiration pneumonia.
