✓ Clinical Rationales

Lithium's narrow therapeutic index (0.6-1.2 mEq/L) makes toxicity a significant concern. Levels exceeding 1.5 mEq/L indicate toxicity. Gastrointestinal (GI) distress, such as diarrhea and vomiting, are often early indicators. Moderate to severe toxicity (levels >2.0 mEq/L) manifests with pronounced neurological symptoms. Ataxia (impaired coordination), a coarse hand tremor (distinct from the fine tremor seen at therapeutic doses), confusion, slurred speech, blurred vision, tinnitus (ringing in the ears), and hyperreflexia (overactive reflexes) are all characteristic. Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, leading to polyuria (excessive urination) with dilute urine, can also develop due to lithium's interference with the kidneys' ability to concentrate urine. A serum lithium level of 2.4 mEq/L is critically elevated, demanding immediate intervention. The pathophysiology involves lithium's interference with sodium transport in nerve and muscle cells, leading to neuronal dysfunction and the observed neurological and GI symptoms. Chronic lithium use can also affect the kidneys' ability to respond to ADH, causing nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
