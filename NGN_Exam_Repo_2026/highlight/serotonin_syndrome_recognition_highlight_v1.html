✓ Clinical Rationales

Serotonin syndrome is a potentially life-threatening condition resulting from excessive serotonergic activity in the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral nervous system (PNS). Sertraline, a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI), increases serotonin levels by blocking its reabsorption in the synaptic cleft. Tramadol, an opioid analgesic, also possesses serotonergic properties by inhibiting serotonin reuptake and promoting serotonin release. The combination of these medications can lead to serotonin accumulation, causing a cascade of symptoms. The highlighted findings are indicative of serotonin syndrome due to the overstimulation of serotonin receptors. Agitation, restlessness, confusion, and disorientation reflect altered mental status. Autonomic hyperactivity is manifested by fever (102.4°F), tachycardia (118 bpm), hypertension (162/94 mmHg), profuse diaphoresis, dilated pupils (mydriasis), abdominal cramping, and diarrhea. Neuromuscular abnormalities include hyperreflexia, spontaneous muscle clonus, shivering, and tremors. These findings collectively suggest excessive serotonin activity affecting the CNS and PNS.
