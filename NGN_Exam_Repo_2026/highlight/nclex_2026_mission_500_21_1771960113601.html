The nurse is caring for a patient in the ICU who is being treated for septic shock. Based on the information provided in the patient's electronic health record (EHR), identify the areas on the patient's body that are MOST concerning and require immediate assessment and intervention.

✓ Clinical Rationales

Septic shock leads to systemic vasodilation and increased capillary permeability, causing maldistribution of blood flow and tissue hypoxia. The areas of most concern include regions where compromised perfusion is most evident or where infection may be originating or propagating. **Mottled Extremities:** Mottling indicates poor peripheral perfusion due to vasoconstriction and reduced blood flow. This is a critical sign of inadequate tissue oxygenation and impending organ dysfunction. In septic shock, this is typically due to the body shunting blood to vital organs, sacrificing peripheral perfusion. **Sacral Wound with Purulent Drainage:** A sacral wound, particularly one with purulent drainage, is a potential source of infection and can exacerbate septic shock. Purulent drainage indicates the presence of bacteria and inflammatory mediators, which can further drive systemic inflammation and worsen the patient's condition. **Distended Abdomen:** A distended abdomen can indicate several serious complications, including ileus, bowel obstruction, or peritonitis. In septic shock, decreased perfusion to the intestines can lead to bowel ischemia and subsequent bacterial translocation, which can worsen the sepsis. The distention itself can also compromise respiratory function by limiting diaphragmatic excursion. **Edematous Ankles:** While edema can have multiple causes, in septic shock, it often indicates increased capillary permeability and fluid shifts into the interstitial space. This contributes to intravascular volume depletion and further impairs tissue perfusion. Ankle edema specifically can also be a sign of venous insufficiency exacerbated by prolonged immobility and increased venous pressure due to fluid overload. **Jugular Vein Distention (JVD):** Although JVD is more classically associated with heart failure, in septic shock, it can reflect fluid overload or right ventricular dysfunction secondary to pulmonary hypertension caused by acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). It indicates increased central venous pressure (CVP) and can suggest that the patient's heart is struggling to manage the circulatory demands of septic shock. This would be less common than the other findings but still can happen. **Clinical Pearl:** Early recognition and aggressive management of septic shock are crucial to prevent progression to multi-organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS). Monitor for subtle changes in perfusion and intervene promptly. **Question Trap:** Be careful not to assume that only obvious signs of infection are critical. While a purulent wound is a clear concern, subtle signs of perfusion deficits (like mottling or edema) can be equally important indicators of shock progression. **Mnemonic:** MAPS: Mottling, Abdominal distention, Purulent wound, Swelling (edema). These signs indicate worsening perfusion in septic shock.
