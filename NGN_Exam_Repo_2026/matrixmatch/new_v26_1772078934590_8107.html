✓ Clinical Rationales

HFrEF is characterized by a reduced ejection fraction (typically ≤40%), indicating a systolic dysfunction where the heart muscle cannot contract effectively to pump blood out. HFpEF, on the other hand, presents with a normal or near-normal ejection fraction (typically ≥50%) but with evidence of diastolic dysfunction, meaning the heart muscle has difficulty relaxing and filling with blood. Both conditions can manifest with similar symptoms like dyspnea and edema, but their underlying causes and treatment approaches differ significantly. Elevated BNP levels are indicative of heart failure in general, as the heart releases BNP in response to ventricular stretch and increased wall tension. An S3 heart sound is often associated with HFrEF due to the rapid filling of a dilated ventricle. Diastolic dysfunction, identified via echocardiogram, is a hallmark of HFpEF. A history of hyperlipidemia, while a cardiovascular risk factor, is not specifically indicative of either HFrEF or HFpEF but contributes to overall cardiovascular disease risk.
