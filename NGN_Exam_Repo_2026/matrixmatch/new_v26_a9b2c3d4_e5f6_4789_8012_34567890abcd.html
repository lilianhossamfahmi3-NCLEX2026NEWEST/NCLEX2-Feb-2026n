✓ Clinical Rationales

Placing the infant in the knee-chest position decreases venous return from the legs and increases systemic vascular resistance, which forces more blood into the pulmonary circulation, improving oxygenation. Administering morphine sulfate helps to decrease pulmonary vascular resistance and also sedates the infant, reducing oxygen demand. Administering 100% oxygen increases the partial pressure of oxygen in the alveoli, which can help to improve oxygen saturation. Monitoring vital signs frequently is crucial for assessing the effectiveness of interventions and detecting any adverse effects. Maintaining a calm environment helps reduce the infant's anxiety and crying, which further decreases oxygen demand and the severity of the tet spell. clinicalPearl: Remember that the knee-chest position is a temporizing measure. Definitive treatment often involves surgical repair of the congenital heart defects.
