✓ Clinical Rationales

A boggy fundus and excessive lochia with clots are indicative of uterine atony, a major cause of PPH. Tachycardia and hypotension are signs of hypovolemia due to blood loss. Pallor and diaphoresis are also signs of hypovolemia. Uterine atony prevents the uterus from contracting effectively to clamp down on blood vessels at the placental site, leading to continued bleeding. Increased heart rate is the body's attempt to compensate for decreased blood volume, while hypotension reflects the failure to maintain adequate perfusion.
