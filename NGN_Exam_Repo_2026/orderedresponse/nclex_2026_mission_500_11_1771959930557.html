✓ Clinical Rationales

The initial priority for a patient with suspected acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is to ensure adequate oxygenation to prevent further myocardial ischemia. Administering oxygen via nasal cannula addresses this need by increasing the available oxygen to the myocardium. Obtaining a 12-lead ECG is the next critical step to rapidly identify any ST-segment elevation or other ECG changes indicative of myocardial infarction, guiding further treatment. Establishing IV access is essential for administering medications and fluids quickly and efficiently. Sublingual nitroglycerin is administered to dilate coronary arteries and improve blood flow, relieving chest pain by reducing myocardial workload. Morphine sulfate is used for pain relief if nitroglycerin is ineffective, and also helps reduce anxiety and myocardial oxygen demand, further stabilizing the patient. Each step is crucial in the acute management of ACS to minimize myocardial damage and improve patient outcomes.
