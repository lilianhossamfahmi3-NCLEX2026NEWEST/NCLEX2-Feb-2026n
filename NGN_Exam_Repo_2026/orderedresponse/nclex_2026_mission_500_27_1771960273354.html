His initial presentation includes altered mental status, severe dehydration, a serum glucose level of 980 mg/dL, and serum osmolality of 340 mOsm/kg. The following interventions are prescribed. Prioritize the order in which the nurse should implement these interventions.

✓ Clinical Rationales

1. **Assess neurological status frequently:** Assessing neurological status is paramount due to the patient’s altered mental status secondary to hyperosmolarity. This assessment provides a baseline and allows for monitoring of changes indicating improvement or deterioration. 2. **Provide supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula:** Patients with HHS often have reduced oxygenation due to dehydration and altered respiratory function. Providing supplemental oxygen ensures adequate tissue perfusion during the initial resuscitation phase. 3. **Administer a bolus of 0.9% normal saline:** The initial intervention for HHS focuses on rapid rehydration to correct the severe fluid deficit. A bolus of isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl) helps to expand intravascular volume, improve blood pressure, and start reducing serum osmolality. 4. **Insert a urinary catheter to monitor urine output:** Accurate monitoring of urine output is crucial to assess renal perfusion and the effectiveness of fluid resuscitation. It helps in guiding further fluid administration and preventing over- or under-hydration. 5. **Initiate continuous intravenous insulin infusion:** While insulin is essential to reduce hyperglycemia, it should be started after initial fluid resuscitation. Rapidly decreasing glucose levels without addressing fluid deficits can lead to cerebral edema and other complications. Continuous infusion allows for controlled glucose reduction. 6. **Monitor serum potassium levels:** Insulin administration shifts potassium intracellularly, which can lead to hypokalemia. Monitoring potassium levels is critical, but it is lower in priority than initial resuscitation and glucose control. Potassium replacement should be initiated as needed based on lab values.
