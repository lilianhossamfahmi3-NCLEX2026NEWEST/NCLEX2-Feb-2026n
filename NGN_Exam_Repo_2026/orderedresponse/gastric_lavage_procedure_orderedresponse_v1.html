✓ Clinical Rationales

Gastric lavage, or stomach pumping, is a procedure used to remove ingested substances from the stomach. It's most effective when performed within one hour of ingestion. **(1) Position the client in the left lateral decubitus position (head down):** This position minimizes the risk of aspiration by utilizing gravity to keep stomach contents from entering the airway. The head-down tilt further aids in preventing aspiration. **(2) Insert a large-bore orogastric tube and verify placement:** A large-bore tube (36-40 Fr) is necessary to effectively remove pill fragments and other particulate matter. Placement must be verified via auscultation while injecting air, pH testing of aspirate (pH should be ≤5), or X-ray if necessary, to ensure the tube is in the stomach and not the respiratory tract. **(3) Instill 200–300 mL of room-temperature saline into the stomach:** Room-temperature saline is used to avoid inducing hypothermia. Smaller volumes are instilled to prevent forcing the ingested substance further into the small intestine. **(4) Aspirate the stomach contents or allow them to drain by gravity:** After instillation, the stomach contents are aspirated using a large syringe or allowed to drain by gravity. This removes the ingested substance and the instilled saline. **(5) Continue the instillation and aspiration intervals until the returned fluid is clear:** The instillation and aspiration process is repeated until the returned fluid is clear, indicating that most of the ingested substance has been removed. Monitor the client closely for complications such as aspiration, esophageal perforation, and electrolyte imbalances.
